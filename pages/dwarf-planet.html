Dwarf planet

A dwarf planet is a celestial body that orbits the Sun and is large enough to be round due to its own gravity, but has not cleared its orbit of other objects (cleared the neighborhood). This means that dwarf planets share their orbits with other bodies of similar, or greater size.

The number of known dwarf planets is not agreed upon, as there are multiple methods of measuring the roundness of objects. According to the IAU and NASA, there are five confirmed dwarf planets in our solar system: Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres. Other objects considered dwarf planets by some are Gonggong, Quaoar, Sedna, Orcus, Salacia and many more.

Pluto is the biggest dwarf planet. It was once considered the ninth planet in our solar system, but after the discovery of the similarly sized Eris and many more potentially round bodies, it was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006. Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres were also subsequently classified as dwarf planets.

With the exception of Ceres, dwarf planets are found beyond the orbit of Neptune. Such bodies are known as trans-Neptunian objects. Most of them are located in an area known as the Kuiper Belt and others are part of other smaller regions, often considered part of the Kuiper belt. Dwarf planet are typically made up of ice and rock and can have moons of their own. Many more dwarf planets are yet to be discovered or proven to be such in our solar system and beyond.

Discovery

Ceres is the first dwarf planet ever discovered. It was discovered in 1801 by the Italian astronomer Giuseppe Piazzi. It was the first object to be discovered in a region between Mars and Jupiter, called the asteroid belt. For a while it was considered a planet before many other asteroids were found orbiting the Sun.

Pluto was discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh. It was considered the ninth planet in our solar system. After the discovery of other bodies beyond Neptune like Eris, the number of planets, if they were recognised as such, could have been as high as a few hundred. Pluto was re-classified as a dwarf planet in 2006. Eris itself was discovered in 2005 by a team of astronomers lead by Mike Brown, Chad Trujillo and David Rabinowitz at the Palomar Observatory. It is the largest known dwarf planet by mass and at the time of discovery was also thought to be larger in size than Pluto.

Critera

The criteria that distinguishes dwarf planets from planets is "clearing the neighborhood". This means that dwarf planets have not cleared their orbits of other objects. Their orbits are shared with other bodies of similar, or greater size, and they are not the dominant object in these orbits.

There are multiple ways of measuring the orbital dominance. One way is Soter's µ, defined as the ratio between a body's mass and the mass of every other body within it's orbital zone. If µ is greater than 100, then the body is a planet. Other more complex ways include Stern-Levison's Λ and Margot's Π. All three methods show a clear division between planets and dwarf planets.

The roundness of a dwarf planet is harder to define. Of the five official dwarf planets, only Ceres and Pluto were visited by spacecrafts. Mike Brown classifies numerous TNOs in different categories of certainty to be round, based on size. According to him, ten TNOs are "nearly certainly" dwarf planets - Eris, Pluto, Makemake, Gonggong, Haumea, Quaoar, Sedna, Orcus, 2002MS4 and Salacia. The list has not been expanded since 2018 and old entries in the list have not been updated. All of the objects in the "nearly certainly" category were thought to be at least 900 km in diameter (2002MS4, Salacia and possibly Orcus are between 900 and 800 km in diameter).

Officially recognised dwarf planets

Pluto

Pluto

Pluto is the biggest dwarf planet and TNO in the Solar System. It is named after the Greek god of the underworld, also known as Hades. After it's discovery, Pluto was considered to be a planet, until 2006, when the IAU categorised it as a dwarf planet. It is composed of mostly nitrogen ice and rock. Pluto has five satellites. The biggest moon - Charon, named after the ferryman of Hades, is so big compared to Pluto, that the barycenter of Pluto lies outside of it and both objects are tidally locked to each other.

Eris

Eris

Eris is the most massive of the dwarf planets and is slightly smaller than Pluto. It's discovery lead to the reclassification of Pluto as a dwarf planet. For a time it had the nickname "Xena" after the main character of an American TV series - the nickname was chosen for it's initial letter X as in Planet X. It's proper name Eris comes from the Greek goddess of discord. It resides in the scattered disc and was the farthest discovered Solar System object until the discovery of 2018 VG18. Unlike other TNOs that appear reddish, Eris is mostly white. One moon is known to orbit Eris - Dysnomia.

Haumea

Haumea

Haumea is an unusual dwarf planet. It takes four hours to complete a rotation around it's axis, causing it's shape to be an ellipsoid. It is named after the Hawaiian goddess of fertility. Haumea has the first known ring system of a TNO. There are two moons orbiting Haumea - Hiʻiaka and Namaka.

Makemake

Makemake

Makemake is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt. It is named after the Rapa Nui god-creator. The dwarf planet has a reddish color from the methane, ethane and tholins on it's surface. Makemake has one unnamed satellite, nicknamed MK2.

Ceres

Ceres

Ceres is the biggest of the asteroids in the asteroid belt and is the only dwarf planet in the belt. It is named after the Roman goddess of agriculture and fertility. It was discovered in 1801 and was thought to be a planet, before other smaller bodies like Pallas, Juno and Vesta were discovered. The surface of the asteroid consists of ice and carbonates. Cryovolcanism activity shapes the surface of the dwarf planet, thanks to which many bright spots in craters and a single mountain (Ahuna Mons) exist.

Potential dwarf planets

Gonggong

Gonggong is the largest known TNO not recognised as a dwarf planet by the IAU. It is named after a Chinese water god with a human head and a serpent body. It is located in the scattered disc and is one of the farthest known objects in the Solar System. Gonggong's surface is red from the likely presence of tholins, similarly to Makemake. It has a single satellite - Xiangliu.

Quaoar

Quaoar is a potential dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt. It is named after the Tongva god-creator. There are signs of water ice and cryovolcanism on Quaoar. Quaoar has one satellite - Weywot.

Sedna

Sedna is a detached trans-Neptunian object and a potential dwarf planet. It is named after the Inuit goddess of the sea. Sedna is a member of a group of objects known as sednoids, which have an eccentricity greater than 0.8 and a perihelion greater than 47.8 AU. Detached objects like Sedna suggest a possible ninth planet orbiting well beyond any discovered object in the Solar System.

Orcus

Orcus is another potential dwarf planet. It is named after the Etruscan and Roman god of the underworld and punisher of broken oaths. Orcus is a plutino just like Pluto and has a orbital resonace with Neptune. It has one known moon - Vanth.

Salacia

Salacia is a TNO and a likely dwarf planet. It is named after the Roman goddess of salt water. Salacia has a single moon Actaea.
